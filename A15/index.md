abstract: |-2
  <p>Spatial computing stands at the heart of immersive, competitive experiences. When reality yields to fast-paced virtual arenas, every millisecond can clutch the win, and every dropped frame can shatter the illusion. Widespread spatial partitioning of diverse virtual realities (VR) using the topologically close-packed <strong><em>A15</em> phase structure</strong> (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>&#x3B2;</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math>&#x2013;<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>W</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W</annotation></semantics></math>) promises to thread new, scalable dimensions of isotropic order into the fabric of the metaverse itself.</p>
  <p>This research advances the development of a trans-metaversal coordination space. Latency-sensitive, full-body VR experiences&#x2014;such as online multiplayer tournaments&#x2014;face significant challenges under current infrastructure, where IEEE 754 floating-point representation errors introduce subtle inconsistencies across different hardware and software environments, impacting responsiveness and fairness <span class="citation" data-cites="Claypool2006">(Claypool and Claypool 2006)</span>. These inconsistencies undermine determinism in distributed systems, and no standard float implementation guarantees identical results across platforms. The proposed solution enforces cross-platform consistency by mapping float positions to deterministic integer identifiers within a crystallographic lattice, providing stable, reproducible behavior.</p>
  <p>Full immersion demands higher fidelity at greater speeds; simply relaying kinematic coordinates for complex interactions (e.g., a 5-on-5 full-body VR match) can result in aggregate data rates exceeding 100&#xA0;&#xA0;&#xA0;s<sup>&#x2212;1</sup> per user.</p>
  <p>The proposed solution addresses these issues by mapping three-dimensional (3D) floating-point coordinates&#x2014;and their often-unused dynamic range&#x2014;to the nearest <em>A15</em>-encoded integer representation. By embedding <em>A15</em> within structured partitioning geometries like the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;honeycomb or the&#xA0;Tetrastix&#xA0;Prism&#xA0;prism and operating within rigorously defined numerically stable scaling regimes, the framework discretizes 3D space while guaranteeing deterministic fidelity relative to its chosen grid resolution. The result is a higher-order representation that is packable, stackable, significantly smaller (often reducing data size by 50% or more compared to standard 32 float vectors), minimizes directional aliasing, maintains compatibility with global measurement standards, and ensures verifiable consistency&#x2014;crucial for responsive, fair, and trustworthy virtual experiences.</p>
  <p>In a quest for instant replay and deterministic fidelity, aligning virtualities with <em>A15</em> ensures every bit of space is part of the living, responsive experience.</p>

  <h1 class="unnumbered" id="executive-summary">Executive Summary</h1>
  <p>The <em>A15</em> encoding framework addresses a fundamental challenge in spatial computing: achieving deterministic, bandwidth-efficient coordinate representation across heterogeneous systems. By mapping continuous 3D coordinates onto a crystallographically-inspired integer lattice, this approach establishes precise spatial representation within the inherently imprecise world of floating-point arithmetic.</p>
  <p>At its core, the framework leverages the unique properties of the <em>A15</em> phase structure (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>&#x3B2;</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math>&#x2013;<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>W</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W</annotation></semantics></math>), a crystal structure with exceptional isotropy and binary-compatible coordinates. Through a carefully designed multi-stage scaling pipeline that prioritizes integer representation during geometric construction, it guarantees bit-identical spatial encoding when operating within defined stable scaling regimes (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>). This eliminates the representation errors that plague floating-point coordinates, ensuring consistent behavior across different hardware, operating systems, and compiler implementations.</p>
  <p>For networked virtual environments&#x2014;particularly competitive VR, collaborative simulations, and distributed computing&#x2014;this translates directly to several practical benefits:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p><strong>Network Efficiency:</strong> Shrinks coordinate transmission size by 50% compared to raw float vectors, enabling higher update rates within limited bandwidth.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Deterministic Guarantee:</strong> Enables verifiable event sequences, accurate replays, and fair competition across heterogeneous client devices.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Geometric Fidelity:</strong> Preserves spatial relationships with minimal directional bias owing to <em>A15</em>&#x2019;s high coordination (13.5) and near-icosahedral local ordering.</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>The framework includes robust tools for verifying numerical stability through histogram analysis, enabling developers to confidently implement deterministic spatial systems while maintaining flexibility in coordinate scale selection appropriate to their application domain.</p>

  <h1 id="sec-introduction">Introduction: Floats, Fairness, and the A15 Foundation</h1>
  <p>Binary floating-point numbers, commonly known as &#x201C;floats&#x201D; and largely governed by the IEEE 754 standard <span class="citation" data-cites="IEEE754-2019">(IEEE 2019)</span>, are the computational workhorses for representing real numbers. They offer a vast dynamic range essential for graphics and science, yet this reach often comes at the cost of precision. Floats are notorious for providing <em>approximately correct</em> answers<a href="#fn1" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref1" role="doc-noteref"><sup>1</sup></a>, representing most values inexactly. These tiny, fundamental representation errors, inherent in their structure (<a href="#eq-float-representation" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="eq-float-representation">[eq-float-representation]</a>), can accumulate and interact, particularly challenging the deterministic consistency required for networked virtual reality (VR) or distributed simulations. This research confronts these challenges head-on, proposing an alternative spatial representation rooted in the unique crystallographic properties of the <em>A15</em> phase structure (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>&#x3B2;</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math>&#x2013;<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>W</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W</annotation></semantics></math>, space group <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math>, No.&#xA0;223 <span class="citation" data-cites="ITCVolumeA2016">(Aroyo 2016)</span>), as illustrated in <a href="#fig-intro" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-intro">1</a>. Furthermore, establishing a robust foundation for spatial representation with inherent numerical stability and structural consistency extends beyond
  immediate interactive fidelity. Such a foundation offers compelling advantages for long-term data archival, application-agnostic interoperability across diverse platforms, and the efficient decomposition and distribution of spatial computations in parallel and edge computing environments.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-intro-floats">The Challenge of Floating-Point Precision</h2>
  <p>Standard normalized binary floating-point numbers derive their value from three components: a sign bit, a biased exponent, and a significand (mantissa). Conceptually<a href="#fn2" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref2" role="doc-noteref"><sup>2</sup></a>, their value relates to: <math display="block" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mtext mathvariant="normal">value</mtext><mo>=</mo><msup><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mi>&#x2212;</mi><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mtext mathvariant="normal">sign</mtext></msup><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>+</mo><mtext mathvariant="normal">significand</mtext><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mo>&#xD7;</mo><msup><mn>2</mn><mtext mathvariant="normal">exponent</mtext></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\label{eq-float-representation}
      \text{value} = (-1)^{\text{sign}} \times (1 + \text{significand}) \times 2^{\text{exponent}}</annotation></semantics></math> The fixed-size significand offers constant <em>relative</em> precision within an exponent range, while the exponent scales the value logarithmically. This design excels at exactly representing powers of two and fractions whose denominators are solely powers of two, but only a finite subset thereof. Consequently, most decimal values and even many simple fractions (e.g., <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>10</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/10</annotation></semantics></math>) are merely approximated <span class="citation" data-cites="Goldberg1991">(Goldberg 1991)</span>.</p>
  <p>While seemingly minuscule, these representation errors can compound, critically affecting reproducibility. In distributed systems like multiplayer games or collaborative VR, this becomes paramount. Identical logical inputs processed on different hardware architectures, operating systems, or even with different compiler optimizations can yield subtly divergent floating-point results. This variance breaks simulation consistency, undermines fairness in competition <span class="citation" data-cites="Claypool2006">(Claypool and Claypool 2006)</span>, complicates debugging and verification, and fundamentally opposes the requirement for determinism.</p>
  <p>These challenges manifest most acutely in latency-sensitive, spatially-complex applications where:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Small coordinate discrepancies can produce visible jitter or desynchronization</p></li>
  <li><p>State consistency must be maintained across heterogeneous client devices</p></li>
  <li><p>Replays and verifiable event sequences require bit-exact reproduction</p></li>
  <li><p>Network bandwidth constraints demand efficient coordinate representation</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Yet, widespread hardware acceleration makes IEEE 754 formats&#x2014;primarily binary32 (32, single-precision) and binary64 (64, double-precision)&#x2014;ubiquitous in modern CPUs and GPUs. Binary32, in particular, remains vital for performance-sensitive applications like game development and VR, establishing a key baseline for efficiency comparisons. This work, therefore, accepts the practical necessity of floats but seeks to structure their use, mitigating risks to determinism via the disciplined partitioning and verifiable scaling offered by the <em>A15</em> framework detailed in <a href="#sec-framework-design" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="sec-framework-design">2</a>.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-intro-a15">The A15 Phase Structure: A Crystallographic Foundation</h2>
  <p>Effectively partitioning virtual space requires a measure of geometric &#x201C;fairness&#x201D;&#x2014;a balance between <strong>isometry</strong> (preserving distances between corresponding points) and <strong>isotropy</strong> (uniformity of properties across different directions). Discretizing continuous space onto a lattice while preserving both ensures that spatial relationships remain consistent and free from directional bias. While regular lattices achieve perfect isometry through translational symmetry, attaining high isotropy is constrained by fundamental geometric principles. The continuous rotational symmetry group in 3D, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>S</mi><mi>O</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>3</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">SO(3)</annotation></semantics></math>, implies that highly isotropic structures should appear locally spherical. However, the imposition of discrete translational symmetry restricts the allowed rotational symmetries to only 1-, 2-, 3-, 4-, and 6-fold axes. This is the essence of the <strong>crystallographic restriction theorem</strong> <span class="citation" data-cites="AshcroftMermin1976">(Ashcroft and Mermin 1976)</span>, which explicitly forbids the global 5-fold symmetry characteristic of highly isotropic polyhedra like the icosahedron (associated with the <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>I</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">I_h</annotation></semantics></math> point group symmetry <span class="citation" data-cites="Coxeter1973">(Coxeter 1973)</span>) in periodic lattices.</p>
  <p>The <em>A15</em> structure (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math>, No.&#xA0;223 <span class="citation" data-cites="ITCVolumeA2016">(Aroyo 2016)</span>) navigates this restriction with notable elegance. It is based on the simple primitive cubic (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>c</mi><mi>P</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">cP</annotation></semantics></math>) Bravais lattice but incorporates a complex basis (or motif) containing two distinct types of crystallographic sites within its conventional unit cell <span class="citation" data-cites="FrankKasper1958 FrankKasper1959">(Frank and Kasper 1958, 1959)</span>, as illustrated in <a href="#fig-cell2" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-cell2">2</a>:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>25% are C12 sites: 12-coordinated, occupying Wyckoff position 2a (e.g., at fractional coordinates <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>0</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>0</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>0</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(0, 0, 0)</annotation></semantics></math> and <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1/2, 1/2, 1/2)</annotation></semantics></math>). Their local coordination environment exhibits characteristics related to pyritohedral symmetry.</p></li>
  <li><p>75% are C14 sites: 14-coordinated, occupying Wyckoff position 6d (e.g., at <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>0</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(1/4, 1/2, 0)</annotation></semantics></math> and its symmetry equivalents <span class="citation" data-cites="ITCVolumeA2016">(Aroyo 2016)</span>). Their local environment resembles a tetradecahedron (a 14-faced polyhedron).</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>This intricate arrangement results in a high average coordination number, calculated as <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>Z</mi><mrow><mi>a</mi><mi>v</mi><mi>g</mi></mrow></msub><mo>=</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>0.25</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mn>12</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mo>+</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>0.75</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mn>14</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mn>13.5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Z_{avg} = (0.25 \times 12) + (0.75 \times 14) = 13.5</annotation></semantics></math>, suggesting a densely connected local structure conducive to efficient packing. The local symmetry around these sites is described by the crystallographic point group <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m\bar{3}</annotation></semantics></math>, order 24). Significantly, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> is a maximal subgroup common to both the full cubic symmetry group <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>O</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">O_h</annotation></semantics></math> (order 48) and the non-crystallographic icosahedral group <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>I</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">I_h</annotation></semantics></math> (order 120) <span class="citation" data-cites="CoxeterMoser1972">(Coxeter and
  Moser 1972)</span>. While lacking the forbidden 5-fold axes of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>I</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">I_h</annotation></semantics></math>, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> retains key 3-fold rotational symmetries present in icosahedral structures. This unique symmetry allows the <em>A15</em> structure to incorporate significant near-icosahedral local ordering, boosting local isotropy considerably compared to simpler cubic structures, all while maintaining the long-range periodicity required by crystallography. The structure also features alternating left- and right-handed local environments around the 6d sites, adding further complexity relevant to implementation (<a href="#subsubsec-limits-handedness" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-limits-handedness">3.3.6</a>).</p>
  <p>Crucially for this work, the canonical definition of the <em>A15</em> structure relies on fractional coordinates inherently suitable for binary representation: <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mn>0</mn><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">0</annotation></semantics></math>, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/4</annotation></semantics></math>, and <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/2</annotation></semantics></math>. These values are exactly representable in base-2 systems. When these fractional coordinates are mapped onto an integer lattice (conceptually, by scaling the unit cell coordinates by 4, aligning with the derivation of the 96-unit baseline in <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>), two characteristic squared site-to-site distances emerge within this integer framework: <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>d</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d^2 = 4</annotation></semantics></math> (yielding distance 2) and <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi>d</mi><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d^2 = 5</annotation></semantics></math> (yielding distance <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msqrt><mn>5</mn></msqrt><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqrt{5}</annotation></semantics></math>). The presence of
  <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msqrt><mn>5</mn></msqrt><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqrt{5}</annotation></semantics></math>, the hypotenuse of a fundamental 2:1 right triangle, connects the structure&#x2019;s geometry directly to the golden ratio <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>&#x3D5;</mi><mo>=</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>+</mo><msqrt><mn>5</mn></msqrt><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\phi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2</annotation></semantics></math>, further reflecting the embedded near-icosahedral characteristics. This intrinsic numerical simplicity and compatibility with base-2 representation ensures that the <em>A15</em> structure can be defined and scaled with precision, forming a robust foundation for the numerically stable encoding framework developed herein (<a href="#subsec-stability" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-stability">2.4</a>).</p>

  <h2 id="subsec-intro-partitioning">Local Discretization Methods: WPH and TSP</h2>
  <p>While the <em>A15</em> structure defines the target lattice points for coordinate encoding, mapping arbitrary continuous coordinates from a virtual environment onto these discrete identifiers requires a well-defined <em>local discretization method</em>. This method effectively defines the boundaries of the region (the &#x201C;cell&#x201D;) surrounding each <em>A15</em> site; any continuous point falling within a given cell is quantized or mapped to that specific site&#x2019;s identifier. This framework primarily considers two such methods, both intrinsically linked to the <em>A15</em> structure, sharing its <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> space group symmetry, and implemented within the accompanying <code>A15.py</code> software (<a href="#fig-wp-ts" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-wp-ts">3</a>):</p>
  <div class="description">
  <p>This research utilizes the geometric polyhedral form of the Weaire&#x2013;Phelan structure as a partitioning method. It divides space using cells of two distinct shapes: a 12-faced pyritohedron and a 14-faced tetradecahedron, arranged in a precise 1:3 ratio <span class="citation" data-cites="WeairePhelan1994">(Weaire and Phelan 1994)</span>. Significantly, these flat-faced polyhedra constitute the Voronoi decomposition for the A15 lattice points; the pyritohedron is the Voronoi cell for the C12 (Wyckoff 2a) site, and the tetradecahedron is the Voronoi cell for the C14 (Wyckoff 6d) site. This relationship makes the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb geometry a canonical choice for discretization, as each cell naturally defines the region of space closest to its corresponding A15 lattice site. These cell shapes correspond topologically to the local coordination environments of the C12 and C14 sites within the <em>A15</em> structure, respectively. This partitioning is notable for its high degree of isotropy, closely mirroring the symmetry characteristics of the underlying lattice. (It is important to distinguish this geometric, space-filling honeycomb from the related but distinct relaxed, minimal-surface structure which famously provided a counter-example to Kelvin&#x2019;s conjecture on foam partitioning <span class="citation" data-cites="Kelvin1887 Kusner1996 WeaireHutzler2001">(Thomson 1887; Kusner and Sullivan 1996; Weaire and Hutzler 2001)</span>.)</p>
  <p>A structurally simpler alternative partitioning method, activated within <code>A15.py</code> via the <code>-stix</code> configuration option. This method employs axis-aligned planar faces, effectively dividing space into cubic blocks centered on the <em>A15</em> lattice sites. While offering computational advantages for certain operations (such as point-in-cell tests) due to its simpler geometry, this approach generally exhibits lower isotropy compared to the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb method.</p>
  </div>
  <p>Crucially, both the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb and the&#xA0;Tetrastix&#xA0;Prism serve as interchangeable local discretization methods within this framework. They define the specific geometry used to quantize continuous space around each <em>A15</em> lattice site. The choice influences the precise shape of these local regions and boundaries, impacting factors like the resulting partition&#x2019;s isotropy and the computational cost of the quantization step itself. However, the fundamental coordinate identifier that is ultimately transmitted or stored remains based on the position within the underlying <em>A15</em> lattice, regardless of which local method is used for the mapping.</p>

  <h2 id="subsec-wp-variants">Clarification on Weaire-Phelan Variants</h2>
  <p>It is essential to emphasize that this research specifically utilizes the <strong>geometric polyhedral form</strong> of the Weaire-Phelan structure as a partitioning method for the A15 framework. This geometric variant consists of flat-faced polyhedra (pyritohedra and tetradecahedra) arranged in a precise 1:3 ratio, constituting the Voronoi decomposition for the A15 lattice points.</p>
  <p>This geometric WPH variant should be explicitly distinguished from the related but distinct <strong>minimal-surface foam approximation</strong> of Weaire-Phelan, which is widely known in the physics literature for providing a counter-example to Kelvin&#x2019;s conjecture on foam partitioning <span class="citation" data-cites="Kelvin1887 Kusner1996 WeaireHutzler2001">(Thomson 1887; Kusner and Sullivan 1996; Weaire and Hutzler 2001)</span>. The minimal-surface variant features curved interfaces minimizing surface area under specific physical constraints, resulting in subtle geometric differences from the polyhedral form used here.</p>
  <figure id="fig-wp-variants">
  <figcaption>Conceptual comparison between the geometric polyhedral form of the Weaire-Phelan structure used in this framework (left) and the minimal-surface foam approximation known from physics literature (right). The geometric form uses flat-faced polyhedra with exact vertex coordinates, while the minimal-surface variant features curved interfaces that minimize surface area.</figcaption>
  </figure>
  <p>The practical implications of this distinction are significant for implementation:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>The geometric WPH used in this framework can be precisely represented using flat-faced polyhedra with exact vertex coordinates, facilitating deterministic computational processing.</p></li>
  <li><p>Point-in-cell tests against the geometric WPH can be implemented using standard computational geometry techniques for polyhedra, avoiding the complexity of curved surface representations.</p></li>
  <li><p>The structural rigidity of the geometric WPH ensures consistent partitioning regardless of cell contents or external influences, in contrast to physical foam structures that might deform based on pressure differentials.</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>In contrast, the minimal-surface WPH would require more complex surface representations, potentially introducing additional numerical challenges contradictory to the framework&#x2019;s determinism goals. Therefore, all references to the Weaire-Phelan Honeycomb (WPH) within this document specifically denote the geometric polyhedral form, not the minimal-surface variant from foam physics.</p>
  <p>This clarification is particularly important because the minimal-surface WPH has gained broader recognition through its architectural application in structures like the Beijing National Aquatics Center (the "Water Cube") for the 2008 Olympics, potentially creating confusion about which variant is employed in this spatial encoding framework.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-comparison-indexing">Distinction from Traditional Spatial Indexing</h2>
  <p>The <em>A15</em>-based partitioning approach presented here relates to, yet fundamentally differs from, traditional spatial indexing techniques commonly employed in databases, geographic information systems (GIS), and various domains of computer graphics (e.g., octrees <span class="citation" data-cites="Finkel1974">(Finkel and Bentley 1974)</span>, R-trees <span class="citation" data-cites="Guttman1984">(Guttman 1984)</span>, k-d trees <span class="citation" data-cites="Bentley1975">(Bentley 1975)</span>; see <span class="citation" data-cites="Samet1990">(Samet 1990)</span> for a comprehensive overview). Conventional indexing methods typically utilize data-driven, adaptive strategies; their partitioning boundaries often adjust dynamically based on the distribution and density of existing spatial objects, with the primary goal of optimizing query performance (such as range searches or nearest neighbor lookups) for that specific dataset.</p>
  <p>In stark contrast, the <em>A15</em> framework imposes a predetermined, regular, crystallographically-inspired structure onto the virtual space itself, largely independent of the dynamic content residing within it. This <em>structure-first</em> methodology prioritizes the creation of a universal, efficient, and geometrically sound fabric for spatial representation and encoding. The key distinctions are summarized in <a href="#tab-comparison-indexing" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab-comparison-indexing">1</a>. The <em>A15</em> approach deliberately trades the dynamic query optimization focus of traditional indexing for significant advantages in predictability, communication efficiency (via compact identifiers that implicitly encode the regular structure), guaranteed numerical determinism (when using stable scaling regimes), and inherent geometric consistency derived from crystallographic symmetry&#x2014;properties particularly valuable for networked virtual environments demanding shared spatial understanding, robustness against floating-point discrepancies, and efficient state synchronization.</p>

However, as discussed (<a href="#subsec-intro-a15" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-a15">1.2</a>), the specific <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> symmetry is significant because it represents a maximal crystallographic subgroup linking cubic

This unique combination allows the <em>A15</em> structure to accommodate its complex basis with distinct C12 and C14 sites, enabling its exceptionally high average coordination number (13.5), which relates to efficient local packing and high connectivity.</p>
  <p>When compared to common space-filling honeycombs and related sphere packings, the <em>A15</em> structure, particularly when coupled with the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb partitioning method, offers further distinctions relevant to this framework:</p>
  <div class="description">
  <p>The body-centered cubic lattice (BCC, coordination number Z=8) and its dual, the bitruncated cubic honeycomb (Kelvin&#x2019;s structure <span class="citation" data-cites="Kelvin1887">(Thomson 1887)</span>), have lower fundamental coordination than <em>A15</em> (13.5), suggesting a less densely connected local environment.</p>
  <p>The face-centered cubic lattice (FCC, coordination Z=12) represents the densest packing of identical spheres <span class="citation" data-cites="ConwaySloane1999">(Conway and Sloane 1999)</span> and is related to the rhombic dodecahedral honeycomb. While achieving maximal coordination for identical points, <em>A15</em> surpasses this average by utilizing two distinct, efficiently arranged site types.</p>
  <p>The geometric Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb, used as a local discretization method, shares the <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> space group and its cell types correspond topologically to the A15 site environments. This ensures the partitioning boundaries naturally align with the symmetries and neighborhood relationships of the underlying A15 encoding lattice, a unique synergy not offered by partitions derived from simpler lattices.</p>
  </div>
  <p>In summary, the unique combination offered by the <em>A15</em> structure&#x2014;its high mean coordination, significant local isotropy via <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> symmetry, a direct relationship to the compatible Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb partitioning structure, and inherently binary-suitable base coordinates&#x2014;makes it exceptionally well-suited for the deterministic, efficient, and geometrically sound spatial encoding framework proposed here.</p>

  <dt>Cell Uniformity:</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>While SC, BCC, and FCC consist of identical unit cells, <em>A15</em> incorporates two distinct site types (C12 at Wyckoff 2a and C14 at Wyckoff 6d) in a precise 1:3 ratio. This heterogeneity enables its exceptional mean coordination without sacrificing periodicity or computational tractability.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Complexity:</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Implementation complexity ranges from the straightforward SC to the more involved <em>A15</em>.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>

  <p>The key insight from this comparison is that <em>A15</em> occupies a unique position in the design space: it achieves exceptionally high coordination (surpassing even FCC) while maintaining perfect compatibility with binary representation&#x2014;a combination not available in other crystallographic structures. This makes it particularly well-suited for applications requiring both high isotropy and guaranteed determinism.</p>
  <p>While simpler lattices like SC offer lower implementation complexity, they suffer from significant directional bias that can adversely affect the fairness of spatial representation. BCC improves upon isotropy but still falls short of the near-icosahedral characteristics of <em>A15</em>. FCC achieves excellent isotropy and packing density but lacks the binary-friendly coordinates essential for deterministic representation.</p>
  <p>The <em>A15</em> structure&#x2019;s combination of high coordination, significant local isotropy, and binary-compatible coordinates provides a robust foundation for the deterministic, efficient, and geometrically sound spatial encoding framework proposed in this research.</p>
  <h1 id="sec-framework-design">The <em>A15</em> Encoding Framework: Design and Implementation</h1>
  <p>The heart of this research lies in translating the idealized crystallographic description of the <em>A15</em> structure (<a href="#subsec-intro-a15" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-a15">1.2</a>) into a practical, numerically robust system for partitioning continuous 3D space and encoding coordinates. This requires establishing an appropriate and consistent scale, mapping continuous coordinates to discrete lattice identifiers, and ensuring the process avoids the pitfalls of floating-point approximation (<a href="#subsec-intro-floats" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-floats">1.1</a>). The framework achieves this through a carefully staged approach to coordinate scaling, primarily operating within an internal integer coordinate system during geometric construction to preserve fidelity and defer floating-point conversion until the final output step. This section details this methodology and introduces the reference implementation, <code>A15.py</code> <span class="citation" data-cites="Risinger2024A15">(Risinger 2024a)</span>, used throughout this work for generation, analysis, and validation.</p>

  <h2 id="subsec-scaling-framework">Core Principle: Internal Integer Representation</h2>
  <p>Applying a discrete lattice like <em>A15</em> to represent continuous space necessitates a rigorous scaling framework. Simply using floating-point coordinates throughout the geometric construction risks introducing the very numerical inconsistencies the framework aims to eliminate. Therefore, the methodology prioritizes calculations within a well-defined internal integer coordinate system, delaying the mapping to inexact output formats until the final step. This preserves the precise geometric relationships inherent in the <em>A15</em> structure and forms the bedrock for numerical stability.</p>
  <p>The essential insight is that while floating-point values inherently approximate most real-world coordinates, integers can exactly represent discrete positions within a lattice. By constructing the <em>A15</em> structure&#x2019;s vertices, edges, and cells using only integer coordinates initially, the framework maintains perfect internal consistency. This integer-first approach enables deterministic construction of the spatial partitioning before any potential floating-point approximation occurs during the final output scaling.</p>
  <p>Crucially, this reliance on an internal integer foundation, particularly when an application deliberately aligns its native coordinate system and units with a chosen stable A15 scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>, <a href="#subsubsec-stability-diff" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-diff">2.4.3</a>), opens pathways for highly efficient quantization. In such aligned scenarios, mapping continuous or application-native coordinates to A15 identifiers may simplify significantly, potentially reducing complex geometric searches to fast integer arithmetic operations like truncation or bit shifts, thereby mitigating concerns about quantization overhead through careful co-design.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-scaling-pipeline">Multi-Stage Scaling Pipeline</h2>
  <p>The conversion from the abstract <em>A15</em> definition to concrete coordinates involves a systematic, multi-stage pipeline implemented within <code>A15.py</code>. This process uses several interacting parameters and fixed factors to progressively build the structure within the internal integer system before final output scaling. The key stages are detailed below, and the parameters are summarized in <a href="#tab-scaling-params" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab-scaling-params">4</a>.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-scaling-prescale">Primitive Definition and Resolution (<code>prescale</code>)</h3>
  <p>The pipeline originates with the definition of the fundamental geometric units associated with the <em>A15</em> basis sites&#x2014;typically the pyritohedra and tetradecahedra related to the Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb method (<a href="#subsec-intro-partitioning" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-partitioning">1.3</a>), or the cubic blocks for the Tetrastix&#xA0;Prism method. Functions within <code>A15.py</code> (e.g., <code>pyritohedron()</code>, <code>tetradecahedra()</code>) apply an internal integer multiplier, the <code>prescale</code> parameter, to the canonical <em>dimensionless fractional</em> coordinates (like <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>0</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn><mo>,</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>2</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">0, 1/4, 1/2</annotation></semantics></math>) defining the vertices of these shapes. This crucial first step converts the fractional values into <em>primitive-relative integer coordinates</em>, establishing the minimum resolution necessary to accurately represent the individual geometric primitives without internal approximation. Default <code>prescale</code> values in <code>A15.py</code> (typically 20 for standard WPH-derived shapes, 24 for TSP-derived shapes via <code>-stix</code>) are chosen specifically to ensure these base vertices land precisely on an integer grid relative to the shape&#x2019;s center.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-scaling-lattice">Lattice Placement (Factor 24)</h3>
  <p>The <code>lattice()</code> function in <code>A15.py</code> replicates these integer-vertex polyhedra periodically across 3D space according to the <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> symmetry rules. It determines the positions for the <em>centers</em> of these polyhedra based on <em>integer lattice vectors</em> <code>xyz</code> relative to an origin offset <code>o</code>. A key element in this placement is the fixed <strong>Lattice Spacing Factor of 24 units</strong>. Within the <code>lattice()</code> function, the calculation <code>(xyz + o) * 24</code> multiplies the integer lattice vector by this factor. This defines the spacing between the nominal lattice points (i.e., the centers of the polyhedra) <em>measured in the integer units established by <code>prescale</code></em>. This factor ensures the correct relative positioning of the repeating units within the overall <em>A15</em> lattice framework.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">Basis Accommodation (Factor 96 Baseline)</h3>
  <p>The <em>A15</em> structure&#x2019;s complexity arises from its multi-atom basis; it contains distinct crystallographic sites (Wyckoff 2a and 6d) not just at the nominal lattice points defined above, but also at specific fractional offsets, notably involving <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/4</annotation></semantics></math> for the 6d sites. To place <em>all</em> required sites onto a single, consistent internal integer grid requires a resolution finer than the 24-unit spacing factor alone provides. The smallest denominator involved is 4 (from <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/4</annotation></semantics></math>). Therefore, the internal integer grid must be conceptually scaled such that one unit step along a primitive lattice vector corresponds to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>4</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mn>24</mn><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">4 \times 24 = 96</annotation></semantics></math> internal integer units along each principal axis.</p>
  <p>This derived <strong>Effective Lattice Unit Factor of 96</strong> represents the fundamental period or effective unit dimension of the comprehensive internal integer grid required to represent the <em>complete</em> <em>A15</em> structure (including its basis) without loss of precision due to these fractional offsets. This factor of 96 establishes the crucial <strong>baseline dimension</strong> relative to which the structure&#x2019;s inherent numerical precision requirements (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>, see <a href="#subsubsec-stability-epsilon-n" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-epsilon-n">2.4.2</a>) are determined and against which the final output scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>) is compared for stability analysis.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-scaling-rescale">Optional Binary Rescaling (<code>rescale</code>)</h3>
  <p>Before the lattice generation stage fully populates the internal integer coordinates, an optional power-of-two scaling factor, <code>rescale</code>, can be applied. This factor is specified via the <code>-rescale</code> command-line flag or implicitly through &#x2018;+/-&#x2019; suffixes appended to shape names in the input (e.g., <code>pyritohedra++</code> implies a rescale factor of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mn>2</mn><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2^2=4</annotation></semantics></math> in <code>A15.py</code>). This <code>rescale</code> factor multiplies the effective size represented by the internal integer coordinates relative to the 96-unit baseline established above. It allows adjustments to the overall size or relative proportions of different generated components <em>before</em> the final output scaling step, operating purely in powers of two within the integer domain. Importantly, the choice of <code>rescale</code> influences the resulting inherent base scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> of the internal geometry.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-scaling-internal">Resultant Internal Integer Coordinates</h3>
  <p>The cumulative effect of the initial <code>prescale</code> (<a href="#subsubsec-scaling-prescale" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-prescale">2.3.1</a>), the lattice placement logic (<a href="#subsubsec-scaling-lattice" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-lattice">2.3.2</a>, establishing the 96-unit effective baseline <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>), and any applied <code>rescale</code> factor (<a href="#subsubsec-scaling-rescale" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-rescale">2.3.4</a>) collectively defines the complete set of vertex coordinates for the entire generated structure. These coordinates exist within a consistent, potentially large-valued, <em>internal integer</em> system referenced against the 96-unit effective lattice baseline. By prioritizing integer arithmetic throughout these construction stages, <code>A15.py</code> preserves the precise geometric relationships inherent in the <em>A15</em> structure, deferring any floating-point approximation until the very last output step. This internal integer representation forms the bedrock for the numerical stability analysis detailed next.</p>
  <p>Furthermore, the explicit control over scale inherent in this pipeline naturally supports hierarchical or multi-scale representations via relative addressing. This allows, for instance, coarse-grained coordinates identifying an entity&#x2019;s global position to coexist efficiently with fine-grained coordinates defining its internal details (e.g., limb articulations relative to a centroid), each utilizing an appropriate A15 scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>) within the same unified structural logic, optimizing both data density and precision.</p>

  <h2 id="subsec-stability">Numerical Stability: Regimes and Validation</h2>
  <p>Achieving deterministic spatial representation&#x2014;ensuring that identical logical operations yield bit-identical results across different systems&#x2014;is a primary motivation for this framework. This guarantee hinges critically on the relationship between the scale chosen for the final output coordinates (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>) and the inherent precision requirements (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>) of the underlying <em>A15</em> geometry, as captured by the internal integer representation (<a href="#subsec-scaling-framework" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-framework">2.2</a>). The framework defines specific, verifiable conditions under which the mapping from the precise internal structure to the output coordinate system can be performed without introducing floating-point approximation errors relative to that internal structure.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-stability-epsilon-d">Global Output Scaling Factor (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>)</h3>
  <p>The primary user control over the final representation&#x2019;s physical scale or resolution is the <code>scale</code> parameter, specified via the <code>-scale=&lt;value&gt;</code> command-line option in <code>A15.py</code>. We denote this crucial factor as <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. It is applied globally by the <code>figure()</code> function <em>after</em> all internal integer coordinates (relative to the 96-unit baseline) have been generated. This <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> defines the mapping from the dimensionless internal integer system to the output coordinate system (typically floating-point). Therefore, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> effectively sets the real-world size represented by one unit of the internal 96-unit baseline dimension, and its specific value is the key determinant of the resulting numerical stability regime.</p>
  <p>It is important to emphasize that <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> does not eliminate the inherent approximation nature of floating-point numbers&#x2014;most real-world coordinates will still require approximation when represented as binary floats. Rather, it establishes a carefully chosen scale at which the <em>specific discrete points of the <em>A15</em> lattice</em> can be exactly represented without such approximation, creating "islands of determinism" within the otherwise approximated float continuum.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-stability-epsilon-n">Inherent Base Scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>)</h3>
  <p>The internal integer geometry produced by the construction process (<a href="#subsec-scaling-pipeline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-pipeline">2.3</a>) possesses an intrinsic precision limit relative to the 96-unit baseline. This limit arises from the specific combination of the <em>A15</em> basis site coordinates (involving factors of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/4</annotation></semantics></math>), the chosen <code>prescale</code> value, and any applied <code>rescale</code> factor. We define <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> as the <strong>inherent base scale</strong> required to represent this specific internal geometry exactly when mapped to a base-2 representation. Conceptually, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> is the finest scaling factor, expressible as a power of two (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/2^N</annotation></semantics></math> for some integer <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>N</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N</annotation></semantics></math>), that allows all generated internal integer vertex coordinates to be represented perfectly as rational numbers without approximation when measured against the 96-unit baseline. It captures the structure&#x2019;s intrinsic geometric precision limit within the binary system used by floating-point numbers. The <code>A15.py</code> script computationally infers
  <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> by analyzing the power-of-two denominators required for the exact rational representation of the generated internal integer geometry relative to the 96-unit baseline.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-stability-diff">Stability Condition and Difference (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math>)</h3>
  <p>Numerical stability is achieved if, and only if, the chosen global output scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>) is commensurate with the inherent base scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>). Specifically, the mapping from the internal integer representation to the output coordinate system is guaranteed to be exact (free from representation error relative to the internal grid) if <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> is a positive integer multiple (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>m</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math>) of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>. The <code>A15.py</code> script quantifies this relationship by calculating the <strong>stability difference</strong>, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math>. Conceptually, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math> measures the mismatch or residual error when checking if
  <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> aligns perfectly with the grid defined by <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>: <math display="block" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mo>&#x21D4;</mo><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x22C5;</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mrow><mtext mathvariant="normal">for some integer </mtext><mspace width="0.333em"></mspace></mrow><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x2265;</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\label{eq-stability-condition}
  \epsilon_\Delta = 0 \quad \iff \quad \epsilon_\delta = m \cdot \epsilon_N \quad \text{for some integer } m \ge 1</annotation></semantics></math> If this condition holds (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>), the framework operates in a stable regime. Otherwise (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>&#x2260;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta \neq 0</annotation></semantics></math>), the scaling is unstable, and approximation errors are necessarily introduced relative to the internal structure.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-stability-regimes">Stability Regimes</h3>
  <p>The stability condition leads to three distinct operational regimes:</p>
  <div class="description">
  <p>This optimal regime occurs when the chosen output scale precisely matches the minimum required inherent base scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta = \epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>). All internal integer coordinates map directly and exactly onto the binary floating-point grid defined by <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N = 1/2^N</annotation></semantics></math> without any approximation relative to the internal structure. <math display="block" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mn>1</mn><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></msup></mfrac><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mo>&#x27F9;</mo><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\label{eq-scaling-binary}
      \epsilon_\delta = \epsilon_N = \frac{1}{2^N} \quad \implies \quad \epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math> This represents the most compact scale that allows exact representation and guarantees determinism. The validation histogram (<a href="#fig-hist" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-hist">9</a>, left) shows a specific pattern of discrete peaks at the exponents corresponding to the inherent A15 geometry.</p>
  <p>This regime occurs when the output scale is an exact positive integer multiple (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>m</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math>) of the inherent base scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x22C5;</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta = m \cdot \epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>). All internal coordinates still map exactly to representable binary floating-point values without approximation error relative to this scaled grid. <math display="block" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x22C5;</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mi>m</mi><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></msup></mfrac><mo>,</mo><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x2208;</mo><msup><mi>&#x2124;</mi><mo>+</mo></msup><mo>,</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>&gt;</mo><mn>1</mn><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mo>&#x27F9;</mo><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\label{eq-scaling-stable}
      \epsilon_\delta = m \cdot \epsilon_N = \frac{m}{2^N}, \quad m \in \mathbb{Z}^+, m &gt; 1 \quad \implies \quad \epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math> This regime maintains perfect representability and determinism while providing flexibility in choosing the overall physical scale. The smallest resolvable difference (Unit of Least Precision, <a href="#subsubsec-notes-ulp" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-notes-ulp">6.4.5</a>) corresponds to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>m</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math> units at the <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> scale, effectively making the ULP equal to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. The validation histogram (<a href="#fig-hist" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-hist">9</a>, middle) displays the same characteristic pattern of peaks as the binary regime, plus additional peaks reflecting the integer scaling factor <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>m</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math>.</p>
  <p>This regime occurs whenever the chosen output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> is <em>not</em> a positive integer multiple of the inherent base scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>. Under these conditions, the precise internal integer geometry cannot be perfectly represented on the binary floating-point grid implied by <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. Rounding errors are necessarily introduced during the final scaling from internal integers to output floats. <math display="block" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>&#x2260;</mo><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x22C5;</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mtext mathvariant="normal">for any</mtext><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mi>m</mi><mo>&#x2208;</mo><msup><mi>&#x2124;</mi><mo>+</mo></msup><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><mo>&#x27F9;</mo><mspace width="1.0em"></mspace><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>&#x2260;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\label{eq-scaling-unstable}
      \epsilon_\delta \neq m \cdot \epsilon_N \quad \text{for any} \quad m \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \quad \implies \quad \epsilon_\Delta \neq 0</annotation></semantics></math> Utilizing unstable scales fundamentally compromises the core benefit of the framework regarding determinism. It introduces representation errors, leading to subtle geometric inconsistencies, non-deterministic outcomes in sensitive calculations, and significant challenges in achieving reliable state synchronization. The validation histogram (<a href="#fig-hist" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-hist">9</a>, right; also <a href="#fig-main" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-main">10</a>) clearly reveals this instability through a broad, sparse, or gapped distribution of denominator exponents.</p>
  </div>
  <p>Therefore, operating exclusively within the <strong>Binary</strong> or <strong>Stable</strong> scaling regimes (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>) is essential for numerical consistency, reproducibility, and guaranteed deterministic behavior.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-stability-validation">Validation via <code>A15.py</code> Histogram Analysis</h2>
  <p>The <code>A15.py</code> script provides not only the means to generate structures based on this framework but also includes a direct, quantitative method for validating the numerical stability regime resulting from any chosen set of parameters, particularly the global output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. This validation capability is accessed via the <code>-bars</code> command-line option and provides empirical confirmation of the stability concepts discussed above (<a href="#subsec-stability" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-stability">2.4</a>). When enabled, the script performs the following analysis within its <code>figure()</code> function:</p>
  <ol>
  <li><p>It iterates through the components (x, y, z) of output floating-point coordinates at the selected <code>scale</code> factor (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>). For each component, it obtains the exact rational representation <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mi>k</mi><mo>,</mo><mi>d</mi><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(k, d)</annotation></semantics></math> using Python&#x2019;s built-in <code>float.as_integer_ratio()</code> method <span class="citation" data-cites="PythonDocsFloatRatio">(Python Software Foundation 2025)</span>. This captures the precise value representable by the float variable, including any approximation introduced if the scaling was unstable relative to the internal grid (<a href="#subsubsec-scaling-internal" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-internal">2.3.5</a>).</p></li>
  <li><p>It then analyzes the denominator <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>d</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math> of this exact rational representation. Since floats operates within a base-2 context, the denominator <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>d</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d</annotation></semantics></math> always simplifies to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>n</mi></msup><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2^n</annotation></semantics></math> for some integer exponent <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>n</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math>. The script extracts this effective exponent <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>=</mo><msub><mo>log</mo><mn>2</mn></msub><mi>d</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n = \log_2 d</annotation></semantics></math>, which conceptually represents <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>&#x2212;</mi><msub><mo>log</mo><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">-\log_2</annotation></semantics></math> of the fractional part&#x2019;s required precision at the output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>.</p></li>
  <li><p>Finally, it visualizes the distribution of these calculated exponents <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>n</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math> across all analyzed vertex components as a histogram (<a href="#fig-hist" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-hist">9</a>). This histogram provides immediate visual feedback on the numerical stability of the chosen configuration.</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p>The shape of this generated histogram directly corresponds to the theoretical stability regimes (<a href="#subsubsec-stability-regimes" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-regimes">2.4.4</a>): a specific pattern of discrete peaks signifies Binary scaling; the same pattern plus additional peaks signifies Stable scaling; and a sparse, broad, or gapped distribution signifies Unstable scaling, revealing the introduction of approximation errors relative to the internal geometry. Furthermore, the script explicitly calculates and displays the stability difference <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math> alongside the histogram (<a href="#fig-main" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-main">10</a>), offering direct numerical confirmation of the regime. This built-in quantitative validation provides objective criteria for selecting scaling parameters that correctly balance spatial resolution, memory efficiency, and the critical requirement for numerical robustness and determinism.</p>

  <h1 id="sec-discussion">Interpretation, Benefits, and Limitations</h1>
  <p>The investigation detailed in the preceding sections confirms the distinctive suitability of the <em>A15</em> phase structure, when employed within the numerically stable scaling framework (<a href="#sec-framework-design" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="sec-framework-design">2</a>), for partitioning interactive 3D spaces. The confluence of its inherent crystallographic properties (<a href="#sec-introduction" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="sec-introduction">1</a>) and the demonstrable numerical stability achievable through disciplined scaling (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>, <a href="#subsubsec-stability-diff" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-diff">2.4.3</a>) yields a framework that is both geometrically sophisticated and computationally practical for demanding immersive applications. This section interprets these findings, highlights the quantifiable benefits derived directly from the framework&#x2019;s mechanics, and candidly discusses practical limitations and engineering considerations relevant to its implementation.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-discussion-interpretation">Interpretation and Core Findings</h2>
  <p>This research establishes <em>A15</em> encoding as a robust structural foundation for coordinated spatial partitioning. The core finding is that by leveraging the specific crystallographic symmetries and binary-friendly coordinates of the <em>A15</em> structure, and by rigorously adhering to the multi-stage scaling pipeline culminating in a stable output scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>), it is possible to create a spatial representation that fundamentally eliminates floating-point representation errors relative to its own discrete grid. This provides a pathway to achieving verifiable determinism in spatial computations, a critical enabler for next-generation networked virtual environments and related spatial computing tasks.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-discussion-benefits">Quantifiable Benefits</h2>
  <p>The <em>A15</em> encoding framework, when implemented correctly within stable scaling regimes, offers several key advantages validated by theoretical analysis and the <code>A15.py</code> <span class="citation" data-cites="Risinger2024A15">(Risinger 2024a)</span> reference implementation:</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-determinism">Guaranteed Determinism via Stable Scaling</h3>
  <p>Arguably the most significant contribution stems directly from operating within the rigorously defined <strong>Binary</strong> or <strong>Stable</strong> scaling regimes (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>, <a href="#subsubsec-stability-regimes" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-regimes">2.4.4</a>). By precisely aligning the chosen output scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>) with the structure&#x2019;s inherent geometric precision requirements (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>, relative to the 96-unit baseline derived in <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>), the framework guarantees that all <em>A15</em> lattice points and derived vertex coordinates map exactly onto hardware binary floating-point formats <em>relative to that chosen stable scale</em>.</p>
  <p>It is important to emphasize that this guarantee applies specifically to the representation of the discrete A15 lattice points themselves; it ensures that the quantized spatial framework remains consistent across systems, but does not eliminate all floating-point variability within applications using this framework. Operations performed between these stable grid points (such as physics calculations, interpolation, or velocity updates) may still use floating-point arithmetic and thus be subject to traditional floating-point variability unless additional measures are taken.</p>
  <p>Nevertheless, this systematic elimination of representation errors for the spatial partitioning grid itself provides a foundation for much-improved consistency across disparate machines, platforms, and even different compilation environments&#x2014;a fundamental prerequisite for reliable state synchronization in networked systems, reproducible physics simulations, efficient network delta compression strategies, verifiable event sequences and replays, and ultimately, fair and trustworthy competitive experiences.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-efficiency">Memory and Bandwidth Efficiency</h3>
  <p>Mapping continuous coordinates onto compact, often integer-based, <em>A15</em> identifiers provides substantial memory and bandwidth savings compared to standard floating-point vector representations (<a href="#subsec-intro-floats" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-floats">1.1</a>). This advantage is particularly pronounced when quantizing explicitly defined or bounded volumes where the full dynamic range and mantissa precision of standard floats represent unnecessary overhead. For a practical example, consider establishing a coordinate system using a stable scale where the basic unit width <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1</annotation></semantics></math> (derived from the 96-unit baseline, <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline,subsubsec-notes-figures" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline,subsubsec-notes-figures">[subsubsec-scaling-baseline,subsubsec-notes-figures]</a>) corresponds to 1.5&#xA0;mm (achieved with <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>64</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta=1/64</annotation></semantics></math>). A 48 integer identifier could then be structured as follows: 3 bits can uniquely address the 8 distinct atomic sites within the A15 conventional cubic cell (2 at Wyckoff 2a, 6 at Wyckoff 6d); allocating 11 bits for the vertical axis provides
  <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mn>2</mn><mn>11</mn></msup><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mrow><mn>1.5</mn><mspace width="0.222em"></mspace><mrow><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext></mrow></mrow><mo>&#x2248;</mo><mrow><mn>3.07</mn><mspace width="0.222em"></mspace><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext></mrow></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2^{11} \times \SI{1.5}{\milli\meter} \approx \SI{3.07}{\meter}</annotation></semantics></math> of range, sufficient vertical headroom for human-scale interaction; the remaining 34 bits, split evenly (17+17) for the horizontal axes, define a square area of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><msup><mn>2</mn><mn>17</mn></msup><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mrow><mn>1.5</mn><mspace width="0.222em"></mspace><mrow><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext></mrow></mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>&#x2248;</mo><msup><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>196.6</mn><mspace width="0.222em"></mspace><mtext mathvariant="normal">m</mtext><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">(2^{17} \times \SI{1.5}{\milli\meter})^2 \approx (\SI{196.6}{\meter})^2</annotation></semantics></math>. Furthermore, this compactness and structural definition offer benefits for long-term data storage and archival, providing a potentially more stable and interpretable format compared to raw floating-point streams. Practical applications, such as the related <code>layoutc</code> project which encodes physical layouts using similar principles <span class="citation" data-cites="Risinger2024Layoutc">(Risinger 2024b)</span>, demonstrate the utility of such compact, deterministic encodings.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-geometry">Geometric Fidelity and Isotropy</h3>
  <p>Leveraging the intrinsic crystallographic properties of the <em>A15</em> structure (<a href="#subsec-intro-a15" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-a15">1.2</a>) imparts beneficial geometric qualities to the spatial representation. Its verified high mean coordination number (13.5) indicates efficient local packing and dense connectivity between neighboring regions. The crucial <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math> point group symmetry provides a high degree of local isotropy by incorporating near-icosahedral geometric elements within a globally cubic (and thus perfectly periodic) framework (<a href="#subsec-intro-a15" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-a15">1.2</a>). This structural integrity, especially when combined with the topologically matched Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb local discretization method (<a href="#subsec-intro-partitioning" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-partitioning">1.3</a>), fosters a spatially uniform or &#x201C;fair&#x201D; representation, helping to minimize the directional biases or artifacts that can plague simpler grid-based partitioning schemes.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-parallelism">Suitability for Distributed Computing</h3>
  <p>The inherent regularity, crystallographic symmetries, and predictable neighborhood topology of the A15 lattice provide a structured substrate well-suited for spatial domain decomposition. This facilitates the distribution of spatial computations across parallel architectures, including clusters, GPUs, or edge networks, potentially simplifying load balancing and data management compared to adaptive or irregular spatial structures. The deterministic nature of the grid ensures consistent partitioning across nodes operating under stable scaling regimes.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-interoperability">Foundation for Enhanced Interoperability</h3>
  <p>By establishing a common, mathematically precise, and verifiable spatial structure, the A15 framework offers a robust foundation for enhanced interoperability. Diverse applications adhering to the same A15 structure, scale, and orientation conventions could potentially exchange, reference, or merge spatial data with greater semantic consistency and reduced ambiguity, fostering cohesion across federated virtual environments or collaborative platforms.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-benefits-validation">Validated Implementation Framework</h3>
  <p>The accompanying <code>A15.py</code> script (<a href="#subsec-implementation-a15py" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-implementation-a15py">2.6</a>) serves as more than just a visualization aid; it is a validated reference implementation and analysis framework. It demonstrates the practical construction of <em>A15</em>-based structures, rigorously implements the multi-stage scaling logic essential for achieving numerical stability (<a href="#subsec-scaling-pipeline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-pipeline">2.3</a>), and provides the quantitative histogram analysis (<a href="#subsec-stability-validation" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-stability-validation">2.5</a>, <a href="#fig-hist" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-hist">9</a>) that empirically confirms the existence and accessibility of the stable scaling regimes (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>). This ensures the reproducibility of the core findings regarding numerical stability and offers a concrete, verifiable starting point for developers seeking to implement or explore the <em>A15</em> encoding framework for their own applications.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-discussion-limits">Limitations and Engineering Considerations</h2>
  <p>Despite its significant advantages for achieving determinism and efficiency, adopting the <em>A15</em>-based partitioning approach involves several practical limitations and engineering challenges that require careful consideration during implementation. These represent addressable design aspects rather than fundamental flaws in the underlying concept:</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-symmetry">Complexity of Arbitrary Rotations</h3>
  <p>Applying arbitrary rotations relative to the lattice axes directly to the integer lattice coordinates used in <em>A15</em> encoding can be intricate, particularly when mapping points onto valid <em>A15</em> basis sites (Wyckoff 2a or 6d) or navigating complex cell boundaries like those in the&#xA0;Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb. Correct implementation necessitates carefully calculated transformations aware of the crystal basis and space group operations <span class="citation" data-cites="ITCVolumeA2016">(Aroyo 2016)</span>.</p>
  <p>In practice, this means that when objects rotate in the virtual space, the mapping of their vertices or bounding volumes to A15 identifiers requires additional computational steps beyond simple coordinate transformation. For arbitrary rotations, the system must typically:</p>
  <ol>
  <li><p>Transform object-local coordinates to world space (standard transform)</p></li>
  <li><p>Apply nearest-neighbor or containment tests to determine which A15 cell contains each point</p></li>
  <li><p>Map these points to appropriate A15 lattice identifiers</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p>The complexity depends on the nature of the rotation and the chosen local discretization method; axis-aligned rotations or operations within simpler partitioning geometries like the&#xA0;Tetrastix&#xA0;Prism may present fewer challenges. Regardless of the approach, adopting a canonical orientation convention (<a href="#subsubsec-limits-handedness" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-limits-handedness">3.3.6</a>) is essential for consistent interpretation and interoperability.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-quantization">Quantization Cost and Design Alignment</h3>
  <p>A practical consideration is the computational cost of quantization&#x2014;mapping arbitrary continuous coordinates to the nearest discrete <em>A15</em> identifier. While general-purpose nearest-neighbor searches in 3D can be computationally intensive, especially with complex cell boundaries (e.g., Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb), this cost is highly dependent on implementation strategy and application alignment.</p>
  <p>For arbitrary coordinates in arbitrary orientations relative to the A15 grid, the quantization process could require:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Point-in-cell tests against multiple candidate cells</p></li>
  <li><p>Distance calculations to determine the nearest lattice point</p></li>
  <li><p>Potentially complex geometric intersection tests for the Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb partitioning method</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>However, as noted (<a href="#subsec-scaling-framework" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-framework">2.2</a>), if an application&#x2019;s coordinate system is deliberately aligned with a stable <em>A15</em> scale (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>), quantization can potentially become a highly efficient process dominated by integer arithmetic operations (e.g., truncation or bit shifts), making performance manageable through informed design choices rather than being an inherent bottleneck. This approach represents a co-design strategy where the application&#x2019;s spatial system is developed with A15 quantization in mind from the outset, rather than being applied as an afterthought.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-integration">Integration with Existing Float-Based Systems</h3>
  <p>Incorporating the A15 framework into existing engines and applications that rely heavily on floating-point arithmetic presents integration challenges. Most modern game engines, simulation environments, and physics systems operate natively with floating-point coordinates and transformation matrices. Introducing A15 encoding requires careful attention to the boundaries between:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Float-based internal physics and rendering systems</p></li>
  <li><p>A15-encoded positions for storage and network transmission</p></li>
  <li><p>Coordinate and state transformations between these representations</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Practical implementation strategies might include:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Using A15 encoding only for network transmission and state synchronization</p></li>
  <li><p>Maintaining dual representations (float for local processing, A15 for verifiable operations)</p></li>
  <li><p>Developing middleware translation layers between engine components</p></li>
  <li><p>Implementing custom physics solvers that operate directly on the A15 grid</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Each approach involves trade-offs between implementation complexity, performance, and the degree of determinism achieved across the entire application pipeline.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-federation">Scalability and Spatial Federation</h3>
  <p>The framework naturally defines discrete, structured zones based on the generated <em>A15</em> lattice extents (controlled by the <code>n</code> parameter in <code>A15.py</code>). Seamlessly extending this model to create massive, open-world environments requires robust mechanisms for managing transitions and maintaining coordinate and state consistency across the boundaries between independent <em>A15</em> zones. Addressing this likely involves developing standardized inter-zone boundary protocols for coordinate transformations (potentially involving scale changes), object state hand-offs, and perhaps authority transfer between simulation domains. Level-of-Detail (LOD) systems <span class="citation" data-cites="Luebke2002">(Luebke et al. 2002)</span> utilizing multi-resolution <em>A15</em> grids (employing coarser quantization for distant or less critical zones, potentially via relative addressing) might also play a role in managing complexity at large scales.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-complex">Encoding Efficiency for Irregular Volumes</h3>
  <p>Using lattice-aligned cuboidal extents for defining <em>A15</em> zones, while straightforward to implement via the <code>n</code> parameter, can lead to inefficient use of the addressable integer coordinate space when representing environments with highly irregular external boundaries, complex internal terrain features (like mountains or caves), or significant voids (e.g., the interior of large, non-rectangular buildings). This may result in significant portions of the allocated integer coordinate range being unused or unreachable within the playable or interactive space. Potential mitigations could include implementing secondary encoding or metadata schemes (e.g., run-length encoding of valid zones along axes, hierarchical spatial masks, sparse data structures like sparse voxel octrees adapted to the A15 lattice), exploring hybrid approaches that combine the regular <em>A15</em> grid with adaptive structures primarily for managing boundary details or sparse areas (though this might reintroduce some complexity), or effectively managing precision and sparsity through hierarchical, multi-scale A15 grids utilizing relative addressing (<a href="#subsec-scaling-pipeline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-pipeline">2.3</a>).</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-limits-handedness">Requirement for Handedness Convention</h3>
  <p>While the overall <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> space group is centrosymmetric (achiral), the specific arrangement of atoms in the <em>A15</em> structure&#x2019;s basis results in alternating left- and right-handed local coordination environments around the 6d sites (<a href="#subsec-intro-a15" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-a15">1.2</a>). This local chirality is implemented deterministically based on lattice position within the <code>A15.py</code> reference code, and is consistent with a standard <strong>right-handed coordinate system</strong> interpretation. However, for interoperability in any practical application, particularly networked ones, all participating systems <strong>must establish and strictly adhere to a shared global orientation convention</strong>, independent of any specific implementation&#x2019;s internal standard. This convention dictates how local chiralities and global axes are interpreted, represented, and transformed, ensuring consistency between different client implementations and, crucially, when interfacing with host environments or game engines that may use different native coordinate system handedness (e.g., left-handed systems common in Unity <span class="citation" data-cites="UnityCoords">(Unity Technologies 2024)</span> and Unreal Engine <span class="citation" data-cites="UnrealCoords">(Epic Games 2023)</span> versus right-handed systems standard in physics and mathematics). Without such a shared convention, mirrored or incorrectly oriented geometry could easily result from exchanging <em>A15</em>-encoded coordinates.</p>
  <h3 id="sec-privacy">Privacy and Security of Tracking Data (PII)</h3>
  <p>The application of this efficient encoding framework to fine-grained spatial tracking data, especially full-body kinematics derived from VR/AR systems, carries significant privacy implications that <strong>must</strong> be addressed with utmost seriousness. Such detailed movement data constitutes <strong>Personally Identifiable Information (PII)</strong> and may qualify as sensitive biometric data under various regulations (e.g., GDPR <span class="citation" data-cites="GDPR-2016">(European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2016)</span>, CCPA <span class="citation" data-cites="CCPA-2018">(California State Legislature 2018)</span>). Handling this data demands rigorous privacy safeguards and unwavering ethical considerations as a <strong>non-negotiable</strong> aspect of implementation. Developers and deployers <strong>must</strong> integrate robust security measures as a foundational requirement. This includes, at a minimum:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Employing strong <strong>end-to-end encryption (E2EE)</strong> for all <em>A15</em>-encoded coordinate streams and associated tracking data during network transmission and persistent storage.</p></li>
  <li><p>Strict adherence to <strong>data minimization principles</strong> (collecting only the data essential for the application&#x2019;s functionality).</p></li>
  <li><p>Implementing transparent <strong>user consent mechanisms</strong> before any tracking begins.</p></li>
  <li><p>Establishing clearly defined <strong>data retention and deletion policies</strong>.</p></li>
  <li><p>Utilizing effective <strong>anonymization or aggregation strategies</strong> whenever full individual fidelity is not strictly required (e.g., for analytics or heatmaps).</p></li>
  <li><p>Ensuring full compliance with all relevant legal and ethical regulations.</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>This data represents individuals and their behavior; it <strong>must</strong> be treated with the highest degree of care, respect, security, and transparency. Failure to do so carries significant legal, ethical, and reputational risks.</p>
  <p>It should be noted that while the A15 encoding framework reduces the raw size of spatial data, potentially making it more efficient to encrypt and secure, this efficiency gain does not diminish the fundamental privacy requirements surrounding such sensitive information.</p>
  <h1 id="sec-outlook">Immediate Potential and Future Prospects</h1>
  <p>The <em>A15</em>-based spatial partitioning and encoding framework, validated for its numerical stability and efficiency (<a href="#sec-discussion" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="sec-discussion">3</a>), offers immediate potential for enhancing current virtual environments and provides a solid foundation for future advancements in spatial computing. Its inherent structural and numerical properties lend themselves to broad applicability, while also opening intriguing avenues for further research and development aimed at refining and extending its capabilities.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-applications">Immediate Practical Applications</h2>
  <p>The practical adoption of <em>A15</em> partitioning across diverse virtual platforms is facilitated by several key features, yielding tangible benefits for various applications available today:</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-apps-coordsys">Cross-Platform Compatibility and Interoperability</h3>
  <p>The underlying <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> space group of the <em>A15</em> structure is centrosymmetric, lacking inherent global chirality <span class="citation" data-cites="ITCVolumeA2016">(Aroyo 2016)</span>. This structural property means the fundamental lattice encoding can be consistently represented within both <strong>left-handed coordinate systems</strong> (common in game engines like Unity <span class="citation" data-cites="UnityCoords">(Unity Technologies 2024)</span> and Unreal Engine <span class="citation" data-cites="UnrealCoords">(Epic Games 2023)</span>) and <strong>right-handed systems</strong> (standard in mathematics and physics) through straightforward external affine transformations. While this simplifies cross-platform integration, achieving unambiguous interoperability absolutely requires establishing and adhering to a shared global orientation convention (<a href="#subsubsec-limits-handedness" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-limits-handedness">3.3.6</a>).</p>
  <p>In practical terms, applications implementing the A15 framework should:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Document the specific handedness convention used (explicitly right-handed or left-handed)</p></li>
  <li><p>Define the precise alignment of A15 crystallographic axes with world-space axes</p></li>
  <li><p>Specify the origin point of the A15 grid relative to world space</p></li>
  <li><p>Include these specifications in any data exchange protocols or standards</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Crucially, the shared mathematical foundation provides a pathway toward application-agnostic spatial understanding, enabling potentially more seamless data exchange between disparate systems built upon the same A15 conventions.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-apps-integration">Integration Strategies with Existing Engines</h3>
  <p>For practical implementation in current game engines and simulation environments, several integration approaches can leverage A15 encoding while minimizing disruption to existing pipelines:</p>
  <dl>
  <dt>Dual-Representation Strategy:</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>This approach maintains two parallel coordinate representations:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Native engine coordinates (typically floats) for internal processing, physics, and rendering</p></li>
  <li><p>A15 identifiers for network transmission, authoritative state storage, and verification</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>The synchronization between these representations occurs at well-defined boundaries:</p>
  <ol>
  <li><p>Convert native coordinates to A15 identifiers before network transmission or state archival</p></li>
  <li><p>Convert received A15 identifiers back to native coordinates for local processing</p></li>
  <li><p>For critical verifications, operations follow the same path through A15 encoding/decoding</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p>This strategy minimizes integration complexity while still gaining the bandwidth efficiency and deterministic verification benefits of A15 encoding.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Middleware Strategy:</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>A specialized middleware layer can handle the translation between native engine coordinates and A15 identifiers transparently:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Intercept network communication and convert coordinates on the fly</p></li>
  <li><p>Provide verification services for authoritative operations</p></li>
  <li><p>Implement comparison operators that account for A15 discretization</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>This approach allows for incremental adoption without significant engine modifications, though it may introduce additional computational overhead at translation boundaries.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Native Implementation Strategy:</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>For applications requiring maximal determinism and efficiency, a more comprehensive approach involves:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p>Implementing custom spatial data structures directly using A15 identifiers</p></li>
  <li><p>Developing physics solvers that operate on the A15 grid with deterministic integer arithmetic</p></li>
  <li><p>Constructing rendering pipelines aware of A15 discretization</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>This approach requires significant engineering investment but can yield systems with strong determinism guarantees throughout the entire application pipeline, not just at network boundaries.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-apps-measurement">Alignment with Global Measurement Standards</h3>
  <p>The explicit output scaling mechanism (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>, controlled via the <code>-scale</code> parameter in <code>A15.py</code>) allows the dimensionless internal <em>A15</em> lattice coordinates (relative to the 96-unit effective baseline, <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>) to map directly and predictably onto standard physical units, such as SI meters or Imperial feet. Critically, selecting a scale factor within the Binary or Stable regimes (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta=0</annotation></semantics></math>, <a href="#subsubsec-stability-regimes" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-regimes">2.4.4</a>) ensures this mapping is exact relative to the framework&#x2019;s chosen resolution. This facilitates interoperability not only between virtual systems but also with real-world measurements, enhancing user comprehension and grounding virtual spaces in familiar metrics. For instance, the recommended baseline scale of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><msup><mn>2</mn><mrow><mi>&#x2212;</mi><mn>6</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta = 2^{-6}</annotation></semantics></math> (<code>-scale=1/64</code>) provides robust sub-millimeter precision while operating within a numerically stable regime, yielding a basic unit width (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1</annotation></semantics></math>) of 1.5&#xA0;mm (<a
  href="#subsubsec-notes-figures" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-notes-figures">6.4.7</a>), suitable for many human-scale interactions.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-apps-relative">Hierarchical Representation via Relative Addressing</h3>
  <p>The framework naturally enables multi-scale representations through relative addressing (<a href="#subsec-scaling-pipeline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-scaling-pipeline">2.3</a>). Applications can utilize this capability to encode coarse global positions efficiently while representing fine-grained local details (like avatar kinematics or intricate environmental features) with high precision relative to a parent coordinate. For example, an avatar&#x2019;s core position could be stored on a moderate-resolution A15 grid, while its complex joint movements are encoded on a much finer A15 grid defined locally relative to that core position. This approach optimizes the balance between data size, spatial range, and the level of detail required for different components of a scene.</p>

Typically expressed as a fraction (e.g., <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>64</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/64</annotation></semantics></math>) or power of two (e.g., <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msup><mn>2</mn><mrow><mi>&#x2212;</mi><mn>6</mn></mrow></msup><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2^{-6}</annotation></semantics></math>).</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p><strong>Inherent Base Scale</strong> &#x2014; The minimum scaling factor, expressible as a power of two (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><msup><mn>2</mn><mi>N</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/2^N</annotation></semantics></math>), required to represent the internal integer geometry exactly when mapped to a binary number system. Inferred by analyzing the generated structure&#x2019;s geometric requirements relative to the 96-unit baseline dimension.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p><strong>Stability Difference</strong> &#x2014; Measures the mismatch between <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> and <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>. When <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math>, the scaling is stable, meaning the output coordinates can be represented exactly in binary floating-point format relative to the internal grid. Calculated as the residual after subtracting the nearest integer multiple of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math> from <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p><strong>Basic Unit Width</strong> &#x2014; The physical dimension corresponding to the 96-unit effective lattice baseline at the chosen output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. Calculated as <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>96</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">96 \times \epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. For the recommended scale of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><msup><mn>2</mn><mrow><mi>&#x2212;</mi><mn>6</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta = 2^{-6}</annotation></semantics></math>, this yields <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><msup><mn>2</mn><mrow><mi>&#x2212;</mi><mn>6</mn></mrow></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>64</mn><mo>=</mo><mn>1.5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1 = 96 \times 2^{-6} = 96/64 = 1.5</annotation></semantics></math> (typically measured in millimeters for human-scale applications).</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>ULP</dt>
  <dd>
  <p><strong>Unit of Least Precision</strong> &#x2014; The smallest coordinate difference or spatial distance along a principal axis that can be exactly resolved by the quantization scheme at a specific scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. Equal to the chosen output scale factor, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>
  <h2 id="subsec-glossary-terms">Technical Terminology</h2>
  <dl>
  <dt>A15</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>The crystallographic designation for the <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>&#x3B2;</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math>&#x2013;<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>W</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W</annotation></semantics></math> structure with space group <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math> (No. 223), characterized by high coordination and near-icosahedral local ordering. Named according to the Strukturbericht notation system used in crystallography.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>&#x3B2;</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\beta</annotation></semantics></math>&#x2013;<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>W</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">W</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Beta-tungsten, the metallurgical name for the A15 crystal structure, originally observed in tungsten but subsequently found in various intermetallic compounds with the general formula A<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi></mi><mn>3</mn></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">_3</annotation></semantics></math>B.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>C12</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>12-coordinated sites within the A15 structure, occupying Wyckoff position 2a, comprising 25% of the basis sites. The local environment around these sites resembles a pyritohedron.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>C14</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>14-coordinated sites within the A15 structure, occupying Wyckoff position 6d, comprising 75% of the basis sites. The local environment around these sites resembles a tetradecahedron.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Determinism</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Guarantee that identical operations produce bit-identical results across different computing environments, regardless of hardware architecture, operating system, or compiler optimizations.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Isotropy</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Uniformity of properties across different directions, indicating how "fair" or unbiased a spatial representation is. Higher isotropy means minimal directional artifacts or biases.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Lattice</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>A periodic arrangement of points in space, defined by translational symmetry. In crystallography, a lattice is characterized by its Bravais type and basis vectors.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Pm\bar{3}n</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Hermann-Mauguin notation for the space group (No. 223) of the A15 structure, describing its complete symmetry operations. The notation indicates: P (primitive unit cell), m (mirror plane), <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\bar{3}</annotation></semantics></math> (3-fold rotoinversion axis), n (glide plane).</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Quantization</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>The process of mapping continuous coordinates to discrete A15 lattice identifiers. This transformation discretizes space according to the chosen partitioning method (WPH or TSP) and scaling factor.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>T</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T_h</annotation></semantics></math></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Point group symmetry (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi><mover><mn>3</mn><mo accent="true">&#x203E;</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m\bar{3}</annotation></semantics></math>, order 24) describing the local symmetry around sites in the A15 structure. This group is a maximal subgroup common to both cubic symmetry (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>O</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">O_h</annotation></semantics></math>) and icosahedral symmetry (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>I</mi><mi>h</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">I_h</annotation></semantics></math>), enabling the structure&#x2019;s high local isotropy within a periodic lattice.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>TSP</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Tetrastix Prism, a simplified partitioning method using axis-aligned planar faces to divide space into cubic blocks centered on A15 lattice sites. Offers computational efficiency at some cost to isotropy.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>WPH</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Weaire-Phelan Honeycomb, a partitioning method using two distinct polyhedra (pyritohedra and tetradecahedra) in a 1:3 ratio, corresponding to the Voronoi decomposition of A15 lattice points. Provides high isotropy but with more complex cell boundaries.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Wyckoff Position</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Standardized designation for sets of equivalent points within a crystal structure, identified by multiplicity and site symmetry. Named after Ralph W.G. Wyckoff, who systematized the classification of crystal structures.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>96-Unit Baseline</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>The fundamental period or effective unit dimension of the comprehensive internal integer grid required to represent the complete A15 structure without loss of precision. Derived as <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>4</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mn>24</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">4 \times 24</annotation></semantics></math> to accommodate basis site offsets of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1/4</annotation></semantics></math> within the lattice spacing factor of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mn>24</mn><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">24</annotation></semantics></math>.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>
  <h2 id="subsec-glossary-params">Implementation Parameters in <code>A15.py</code></h2>
  <dl>
  <dt><code>prescale</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Internal integer multiplier applied to shape primitives, establishing primitive resolution (defaults: 20 for WPH, 24 for TSP). Converts base fractional coordinates to integer vertices relative to shape center.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>rescale</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Optional power-of-two scaling applied before generation, adjusting size relative to the 96-unit baseline. Specified via <code>-rescale</code> flag or implicitly through &#x2018;+/-&#x2019; suffixes appended to shape names (e.g., <code>pyritohedra++</code> implies a rescale factor of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mn>2</mn><mn>2</mn></msup><mo>=</mo><mn>4</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">2^2=4</annotation></semantics></math>).</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>scale</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Equivalent to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>, the final global scaling factor applied after generation to map internal integers to output units. Specified via the <code>-scale=&lt;value&gt;</code> command-line option.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>n</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Controls lattice extent: integer specifies cuboid dimensions (number of lattice cells along each axis); float specifies spherical radius cutoff based on lattice vector magnitude relative to the origin.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>-bars</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Visualization option enabling histogram analysis of floating-point denominators to validate numerical stability. The resulting histogram pattern directly indicates the stability regime (<math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math> or <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>&#x2260;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta \neq 0</annotation></semantics></math>).</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>-stix</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Configuration option activating the TSP partitioning method instead of the default WPH geometry. Trades some isotropy for computational efficiency in point-in-cell tests.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>-pop</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Option to display the visualization in a pop-up window. Can be specified as <code>-pop</code> or with a specific command like <code>-pop=open</code>.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt><code>-savefig</code></dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Option to save the visualization to a file. Default filename is <code>savefig.png</code> unless specified otherwise.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>
  <h2 id="subsec-glossary-integration">Integration Strategies</h2>
  <dl>
  <dt>Dual-Representation</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Integration approach maintaining two parallel coordinate representations: native engine coordinates (typically floats) for internal processing and A15 identifiers for network transmission and verification. Minimizes integration complexity while providing bandwidth and determinism benefits at interface boundaries.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Middleware</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Integration approach implementing a translation layer between an existing engine and A15 encoding. Transparently converts coordinates without requiring significant engine modifications, facilitating incremental adoption.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Native Implementation</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Integration approach building spatial data structures and physics directly on A15 identifiers, maximizing determinism throughout the entire pipeline. Requires more engineering investment but provides the strongest guarantees.</p>
  </dd>
  <dt>Relative Addressing</dt>
  <dd>
  <p>Technique using hierarchical A15 grids at different scales, with fine-grained coordinates defined relative to a parent object&#x2019;s position. Enables efficient multi-scale representation while maintaining precision where needed.</p>
  </dd>
  </dl>

  <h2 id="subsec-practical-implementation">Practical Implementation Considerations</h2>
  <p>Beyond the theoretical foundation and validation tools provided in this research, several practical considerations merit attention for developers seeking to implement the A15 framework in real-world applications:</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-impl-performance">Performance Optimization Strategies</h3>
  <p>While comprehensive benchmarking remains an area for future work (<a href="#subsubsec-outlook-performance" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-outlook-performance">4.2.1</a>), preliminary experience suggests several approaches to optimize A15 implementation performance:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p><strong>Aligned Coordinate Systems:</strong> Designing application coordinate systems to align with the A15 grid allows quantization to be implemented as simple integer truncation rather than complex geometric tests.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Caching A15 Identifiers:</strong> For static objects or slowly changing environments, pre-computing and caching A15 identifiers can amortize quantization costs.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Hierarchical Spatial Partitioning:</strong> Combining the A15 grid with higher-level partitioning schemes (e.g., spatial hashing) can accelerate nearest-neighbor searches for quantization.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Simpler Cell Geometry for Performance-Critical Paths:</strong> Using the TSP partitioning method (<a href="#subsec-intro-partitioning" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-partitioning">1.3</a>) for paths requiring frequent quantization trading some isotropy for computational efficiency.</p></li>
  </ul>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-impl-adoption">Incremental Adoption Path</h3>
  <p>Rather than attempting a complete transition to A15-based spatial representation, many applications will benefit from an incremental adoption strategy:</p>
  <ol>
  <li><p><strong>Network Transmission Only</strong> - Use A15 encoding solely for transmitting position updates over the network, while maintaining traditional floating-point coordinates for all internal processing.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Authoritative State Recording</strong> - Extend A15 usage to authoritative state storage, enabling deterministic replay and verification capabilities.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Critical System Integration</strong> - Selectively implement A15-aware subsystems for components where determinism is most crucial (e.g., collision detection).</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Comprehensive Integration</strong> - Develop fully A15-native physics and simulation systems if warranted by application requirements.</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p>This phased approach allows developers to gain immediate benefits from A15 encoding (bandwidth reduction, improved network consistency) while deferring more complex integration challenges until warranted by specific application needs.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-impl-testing">Testing and Verification</h3>
  <p>Implementing a system that guarantees determinism requires rigorous testing approaches:</p>
  <ul>
  <li><p><strong>Cross-Platform Verification:</strong> Test A15-encoded operations across different hardware, operating systems, and compiler settings to verify bit-exact results.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Stability Regime Validation:</strong> For any chosen scale, verify that <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta = 0</annotation></semantics></math> using techniques similar to the histogram analysis in <code>A15.py</code> (<a href="#subsec-stability-validation" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-stability-validation">2.5</a>).</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Boundary Case Testing:</strong> Thoroughly test edge cases near cell boundaries where quantization decisions might be sensitive to implementation details.</p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Deterministic Replay:</strong> Validate that identical initial conditions and inputs reliably produce identical event sequences when using A15 encoding.</p></li>
  </ul>
  <p>These testing approaches help ensure that the theoretical determinism guarantees of the A15 framework translate into practical consistency in deployed applications.</p>
  <h2 id="subsec-supplementary-notes">Supporting Notes and Clarifications</h2>
  <p>Further details, data, and clarifications related to this research are provided below.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-bandwidth">Bandwidth Calculation Basis</h3>
  <p>The discussion regarding network bandwidth requirements (<a href="#subsubsec-benefits-efficiency" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-benefits-efficiency">3.2.2</a>) utilizes a baseline calculation (<a href="#eq-bandwidth-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="eq-bandwidth-baseline">[eq-bandwidth-baseline]</a>) assuming a single avatar with 20 tracked joints, each transmitting 3D position (3x 32 floats) and an orientation quaternion (4x 32 floats) at a 15&#xA0;Hz update rate (67.2&#xA0;&#xA0;&#xA0;s<sup>&#x2212;1</sup>). This kinematic component typically represents only a fraction of the total network traffic in complex interactive applications.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-memory">Memory Efficiency Context</h3>
  <p>The estimate of <strong>50% or more</strong> memory and bandwidth savings (<a href="#eq-efficiency-memory" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="eq-efficiency-memory">[eq-efficiency-memory]</a>, <a href="#subsubsec-benefits-efficiency" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-benefits-efficiency">3.2.2</a>) compares storing 3D coordinates using compact integer <em>A15</em> identifiers versus raw 32 floating-point vectors. A representative scenario assumes a 48 integer representation per 3D point for the <em>A15</em> identifier (balancing range and precision) compared to <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mn>3</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mn>32</mn><mspace width="0.167em"></mspace><mtext mathvariant="normal">bit</mtext><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn><mspace width="0.167em"></mspace><mtext mathvariant="normal">bit</mtext></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">3 \times 32\,\text{bit} = 96\,\text{bit}</annotation></semantics></math> for three standard floats. This efficiency gain is most pronounced when quantizing bounded volumes where the extreme dynamic range and full mantissa precision of floats are not required. The exact saving achieved depends on the application&#x2019;s required spatial extent, desired intra-cell resolution (potentially managed via relative addressing, <a href="#subsubsec-apps-relative" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-apps-relative">4.1.4</a>), and the chosen bit depth for the <em>A15</em> identifier.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-geometry">Geometric Data Availability</h3>
  <p>Tables containing the precise internal integer vertex coordinates (relative to shape centers at the relevant <code>prescale</code> value) for the fundamental polyhedra (pyritohedra with various <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mi>h</mi><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h</annotation></semantics></math> parameters, tetradecahedra) generated by <code>A15.py</code> functions are available within the code repository (<a href="#subsec-replication" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-replication">6.1</a>), allowing independent verification of geometric constructions.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-h75">Pyritohedra Parameter for Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb Geometry</h3>
  <p>As implemented in <code>A15.py</code>, invoking the <code>pyritohedron()</code> function with the specific height parameter <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>h</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>7</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">h=7/5</annotation></semantics></math> yields internal integer coordinates that, after appropriate scaling and placement by <code>lattice()</code>, correspond precisely to the vertex coordinates defining the pyritohedral cells within the geometric Weaire&#x2013;Phelan&#xA0;Honeycomb partition used in this framework (<a href="#subsec-intro-partitioning" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-intro-partitioning">1.3</a>).</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-ulp">Unit of Least Precision (ULP) Definition</h3>
  <p>Within this framework, when operating at a specific Binary or Stable output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> (<a href="#subsubsec-stability-regimes" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-stability-regimes">2.4.4</a>), the <strong>Unit of Least Precision (ULP)</strong> represents the smallest coordinate difference or spatial distance along a principal axis that can be exactly resolved by the quantization scheme. This ULP corresponds directly to an integer difference of 1 in the underlying <em>internal integer</em> coordinate system (relative to the 96-unit baseline, <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>) before the final scaling by <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> is applied. Therefore, the physical size of the ULP is precisely equal to the chosen output scale factor, <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math>. Features, movements, or discrepancies smaller than <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> cannot be distinctly represented by the encoding at that scale. Selecting an appropriate <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> involves balancing the desired spatial resolution (ULP) against the overall spatial
  range achievable within a fixed-bit integer representation chosen for the <em>A15</em> identifier, potentially leveraging relative addressing (<a href="#subsubsec-apps-relative" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-apps-relative">4.1.4</a>) to manage this trade-off across different parts of a scene.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-adaptability">Framework Adaptability</h3>
  <p>While this research focuses intensely on the <em>A15</em> phase structure due to its compelling combination of advantageous properties for deterministic spatial encoding, the underlying <code>A15.py</code> software framework possesses inherent adaptability. Key components, particularly the <code>lattice()</code> function for replicating geometric units according to symmetry rules and the visualization tools within the <code>figure()</code> function, could be modified or extended to generate and visualize other crystal lattice types or different space-filling structures, offering a versatile platform for broader geometric exploration, albeit likely requiring non-trivial adaptation of the core geometric and scaling logic.</p>
  <h3 id="subsubsec-notes-figures">Interpretation of Figure Annotations</h3>
  <p>Annotations visible in figures generated by <code>A15.py</code> with the <code>-bars</code> option (e.g., <a href="#fig-main" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-main">10</a>) directly illustrate key stability concepts discussed in <a href="#subsec-stability" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsec-stability">2.4</a>. The calculated value <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1</annotation></semantics></math> (labeled &#x201C;basic unit width&#x201D; or similar in some outputs) shown in the histogram sidebar represents the physical dimension corresponding to the chosen output scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> applied to the fundamental internal lattice dimension (the 96-unit baseline, <a href="#subsubsec-scaling-baseline" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-scaling-baseline">2.3.3</a>). For the unstable scale <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>96</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta=1/96</annotation></semantics></math> shown in <a href="#fig-main" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-main">10</a>, this results in <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>96</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mn>1.0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1 = 96 \times (1/96) = 1.0</annotation></semantics></math> (assuming millimeters as the base unit, thus 1.0&#xA0;mm). In contrast, the recommended stable scale
  <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>64</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta = 1/64</annotation></semantics></math> yields <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>N</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>96</mn><mo>&#xD7;</mo><mrow><mo stretchy="true" form="prefix">(</mo><mn>1</mn><mi>/</mi><mn>64</mn><mo stretchy="true" form="postfix">)</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mn>1.5</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N_1 = 96 \times (1/64) = 1.5</annotation></semantics></math> (or 1.5&#xA0;mm, <a href="#subsubsec-apps-measurement" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="subsubsec-apps-measurement">4.1.3</a>). The displayed <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta</annotation></semantics></math> value explicitly confirms the calculated stability difference for the configuration (e.g., <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x394;</mi></msub><mo>&#x2248;</mo><mn>0.0104</mn><mo>&#x2260;</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\Delta \approx \num{0.0104} \neq 0</annotation></semantics></math> for the unstable case in <a href="#fig-main" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig-main">10</a>, confirming <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>&#x3B4;</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_\delta</annotation></semantics></math> is not an integer multiple of <math display="inline" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><msub><mi>&#x3F5;</mi><mi>N</mi></msub><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon_N</annotation></semantics></math>), providing direct numerical validation alongside the visual histogram representation.</p>
